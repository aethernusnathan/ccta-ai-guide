CCTA · FFRCT · PLAQUE AI · INDEPENDENT CLINICAL REFERENCE · 2026

Coronary CT angiography is no longer just anatomy.

AI now extracts physiology and plaque biology from the same scan — without catheterisation, adenosine, or extra radiation. That changes what a CCTA report can mean for your patient.

WHY
♥
The diagnostic gap
Obstructive stenosis on CCTA does not equal ischaemia. Up to 40% of lesions that look significant on anatomy are haemodynamically non-significant when tested invasively. Sending those patients to the cath lab exposes them to risk and cost without benefit.
⚡
The prevention gap
MACE risk lives in plaque composition, not stenosis grade. A non-obstructive lesion with high lipid-rich necrotic core or pericoronary inflammation can rupture before it ever causes symptoms. Conventional reporting misses it entirely.
→
The opportunity
AI-derived FFRCT and plaque quantification answer both gaps from a scan you are already ordering. NICE, AHA, SCCT, and ACC have all issued supporting guidance. Reimbursement in the US is now Category I for both (CPT 75580 and 75577 from Jan 2026).
HOW IT WORKS
01
CT-FFR — AI-derived fractional flow reserve

The CCTA DICOM is sent to an AI platform. A 3D coronary tree is segmented and a computational fluid dynamics (CFD) or deep-learning model simulates hyperaemic blood flow without adenosine.

The output is a continuous pressure ratio (0–1.0) at any point distal to a stenosis under simulated stress. A value ≤ 0.80 is haemodynamically significant — the same threshold used in invasive FFR.

Two platforms hold FDA and CE clearance — the relevant bar for US and European practice: HeartFlow FFRCT and Keya DEEPVESSEL FFR. Five additional platforms are cleared only in Asia (China NMPA, South Korea MFDS) and are listed in SCCT-SCAI-ACC 2026 Table 1 for completeness but are not available in US or EU markets. Cleerly's ISCHEMIA module provides an ischaemia probability score, not a continuous FFR value — classified outside CT-FFR scope by the 2026 consensus.

02
AI plaque quantification — beyond stenosis

Cleared platforms (Cleerly, HeartFlow, Elucid, Caristo, Artrya, Circle CVI) segment and characterise atherosclerotic plaque from the same CCTA. Outputs include total plaque volume, calcified burden, non-calcified plaque (NCP), and — depending on platform — lipid-rich composition (LRNC), pericoronary fat attenuation index (FAI), or histology-based subtypes.

Caristo's FAI captures coronary inflammation even in arteries with < 50% stenosis — a risk signal invisible on standard CCTA. Elucid is the only cleared tool with histological validation of plaque subtypes (CT-VH™). HeartFlow Next Gen Plaque (Sep 2025) adds a 3D visualisation with population nomogram.

DECIDE registry: AI plaque staging changed clinical management in > 50% of cases and reduced LDL by 18.7 mg/dL at 90 days.

WHAT TO DO
1
Identify the right patient
CT-FFR is appropriate for stable chest pain with a 50–90% stenosis on CCTA (SCCT-SCAI-ACC 2026, LOE A). Not for LM ≥ 50%, CTO, stents, or vessels < 1.8 mm. AI plaque analysis is appropriate for any patient with visible plaque on CCTA for risk stratification (ACC 2025) — not all-comers.
2
Acquire the scan correctly
Nitroglycerin 400–800 mcg SL immediately pre-scan is mandatory for FFRCT submission. Heart rate < 65 bpm. Slice thickness ≤ 0.625 mm reconstructed. Aortic root attenuation 300–450 HU. See the Acquisition protocol tab for the full checklist.
3
Choose the right platform for the clinical question
Use the Clinical scenarios tab to match your question to platforms with published evidence in that setting. Use the All vendors tab to compare regulatory clearances, COI disclosures, and capabilities. Not every cleared platform fits every use case.
4
Interpret in clinical context
CT-FFR ≤ 0.80 identifies haemodynamically significant lesions. A grey zone of 0.75–0.85 warrants integration with symptoms and imaging quality. Plaque outputs inform statin intensity and monitoring frequency — they do not replace clinical judgement or the treating physician's responsibility.

FINANCIAL — How to verify payer coverage
The payer universe
~650 US payers — but you don't need all of them
There are roughly 650 commercial payers in the US, plus Medicare, Medicare Advantage plans, and Medicaid programs in each state. Your top 5 payers by volume typically account for 70–80% of your commercial revenue, and your top 10 cover 85–90%.

The long tail of ~640 smaller payers largely follows the top 10 with a 12–18 month lag. Focus coverage verification on your top 10. Do not sign a vendor contract or project revenue until you have confirmed coverage status for each of those 10.
First move
Pull your payer mix report before anything else
Ask your revenue cycle team for a payer mix report ranked by annual revenue — not by claim volume. You want to know which payers represent the most dollars, not just the most patients.

Take the top 10. Those are the only payers that matter for initial coverage verification. Do not start any other research steps until you have this list.
What "Category I" means commercially
CMS coverage ≠ commercial coverage
Category I status means CMS covers it under Medicare OPPS nationally — that's it. Commercial payers are not automatically bound. Each runs its own medical policy committee and issues independent coverage determinations.

Most major commercials reference Medicare fee schedules for rates (paying X% of Medicare). But they make completely independent coverage decisions — and typically lag CMS by 12–24 months.
4-layer verification framework
1
Ask your vendor's reimbursement team first (fastest path)
Most vendors maintain payer coverage maps by state and have dedicated reimbursement staff who track policy updates in real time. This is the fastest starting point — ask your vendor rep: "Can you show me the current coverage policy status for 75580 and 75577 for our top 10 payers by volume in our region?"

Maturity varies: some vendors have established reimbursement support teams; others are earlier-stage. For 75577 (Category I effective Jan 2026), payer adoption is still building across all vendors — use the vendor map as a starting point, then verify independently with the payer portals in step 2.
2
Check payer medical policy portals directly
Every major payer publishes its medical policies online. If no policy is found, the code is generally not covered or is processed as investigational — neither means you can bill freely.

Prior authorization — practical guidance
Plan for it, especially in year 1
CPT 75580 has required prior auth from some commercial payers historically. With Category I status this is easing — Cigna removed prior auth requirements for FFR-CT in 2025. 75577 is newer, so expect more friction in 2026.

Ask each vendor whether they offer prior auth support services — this varies by vendor maturity. Prior auth support from the vendor can meaningfully reduce the burden on your revenue cycle team, especially in the first 90 days.

Track denial reasons weekly in the first 90 days. The most common deniable reasons: missing prior auth documentation, incorrect ICD-10 pairing, and payer "investigational" policy not yet updated to reflect Category I status. Each is appealable — but appeals take staff time.
Medicare Advantage
Separate from traditional Medicare
Medicare Advantage (MA) plans are commercial plans contracted with CMS — they are not traditional Medicare and can impose their own prior auth requirements and coverage limitations even for Category I codes.

If MA represents a significant share of your payer mix, treat each MA plan as a separate commercial payer for coverage verification purposes. Major MA plans (UHC Medicare Advantage, Humana Medicare Advantage, Aetna MA) often follow traditional Medicare coverage but with their own prior auth overlays.
FINANCIAL — Cost & financial modeling
Cloud per-case model (most vendors)
Variable cost, zero capital outlay
HeartFlow, Cleerly, Keya, Elucid, Artrya, and Caristo all use cloud per-case pricing. The institution pays a per-submission fee to the vendor; CPT reimbursement partially or fully offsets this depending on payer mix and volume. Vendor per-case fees are not publicly disclosed — request detailed pricing from each vendor and model net margin before contracting.

Key variable: the ratio of fully covered (Category I) to denied or non-covered cases determines realized net margin. Model at low, mid, and high annual case volume projections.
On-premise model (Circle CVI)
Fixed cost, volume-independent margin
Circle CVI cvi42 uses a platform license model. Once licensed, per-case marginal cost is near zero — the institution retains the full CPT 75577 reimbursement on every covered case. Capital: server hardware if not using existing cvi42. Software: annual license fee.

Break-even is reached faster at institutions already running cvi42 (1,700+ hospitals globally). On-premise also eliminates PHI-off-site risk, which may have additional CFO value if your institution carries elevated cyber risk posture or strict data residency requirements.
Dual-platform billing
~$1,851 from one scan — one vendor
Combined billing of ~$1,851 per eligible CCTA case is possible when using a single platform with confirmed FDA clearance for both CPT 75580 (CT-FFR) and 75577 (plaque analysis). This simplifies the workflow: one vendor contract, one DICOM submission, two billable codes from one scan.

As of June 2026, confirm which vendors in your shortlist hold cleared status for both codes — FDA clearance scope varies and changes; verify in the public 510(k) database at procurement. Note: SCCT 2026 classifies Cleerly ISCHEMIA outside CT-FFR scope, which may affect 75580 payer coverage eligibility for that product specifically.
Use realistic covered-case assumptions
Do not model 100% collection rate
Build in conservative covered-case percentages for year 1:

75580: 70–80% covered-case rate (varies by payer mix)
75577: 40–60% covered-case rate (too new for full commercial adoption)
Denial overhead: budget 1–2 FTE-hours per denial for first 90 days

Common denial reasons: missing prior auth, incorrect ICD-10 pairing, payer "investigational" policy not yet updated to reflect Category I status. Each is appealable — but appeals take staff time. Review denial reasons weekly and build appeal templates early.
TC/26 split billing
Facility and physician bill separately
75580 and 75577 both bill as split technical/professional components:

TC (technical component): billed by the facility on UB-04 (hospital outpatient claim)
26 (professional component): billed by the interpreting physician on CMS-1500

Both must reference the same date of service and patient encounter. Ensure your interpreting cardiologists and radiologists understand the billing pathway — some payers require specific subspecialty credential documentation with the 26 modifier. Coordinate with your physician group or hospitalist billing team to avoid duplicate billing errors.

IT — DICOM workflow: cloud vendors
1
CT acquisition & reconstruction
CCTA acquired per protocol (≤0.625 mm slice thickness, ECG-gated, contrast within target HU). DICOM series generated on CT workstation. No changes to existing CT workflow — the AI pipeline starts after reconstruction is complete and the series is pushed to PACS.
2
PACS routes series to vendor DICOM endpoint
A DICOM routing rule (configured once at implementation) automatically sends the designated CCTA series from PACS to the vendor's DICOM receiver (AE Title). This is a standard DICOM C-Store operation — same mechanism used to send studies between PACS nodes. Study size: 500 MB–2 GB typical. Transit: TLS-encrypted DICOM or HTTPS. No manual file handling, no staff action required per case after setup.
3
AI processing in vendor cloud
Vendor ingests the series, runs coronary segmentation, and applies the CT-FFR or plaque AI model on their cloud infrastructure. HeartFlow: typically 45–90 min (priority submission available for urgent cases). Other vendors: similar range. Processing time is independent of your local infrastructure once the DICOM upload completes — a slow upload only delays the start, not processing speed.
4
Report returned to your PACS
AI report delivered as a DICOM Structured Report (SR) and/or PDF, pushed back to your PACS via a preconfigured return DICOM C-Store. The DICOM SR appears in any DICOM-compliant viewer without additional software installation on reading workstations.

Critical configuration point: the return DICOM send (vendor → your PACS) must be configured separately from the forward send (your PACS → vendor). These are two independent DICOM associations — omitting the return path is the most common setup error (see Failure Modes below).
5
Physician interpretation & billing trigger
Interpreting physician reviews AI output in PACS viewer or vendor portal and issues a signed report. CPT 75580 or 75577 billing is initiated from RIS/EHR by the revenue cycle team — the AI platform does not auto-generate charges. The CDM must have 75580/75577 configured as chargeable items linked to the imaging encounter type before this step works.
On-premise workflow (Circle CVI cvi42)
1
CT acquisition & reconstruction
Identical to cloud workflow — CCTA acquired per protocol, DICOM series generated on CT workstation and pushed to PACS.
2
PACS routes to local cvi42 server AE Title
A DICOM routing rule sends the CCTA series from PACS to the local cvi42 server's AE Title. Data stays entirely within the hospital network — no internet transit for the DICOM study. This routing rule is configured once at implementation, identical to cloud vendors from a PACS configuration perspective.
3
Local GPU processes plaque analysis
The cvi42 server's GPU processes the plaque analysis entirely on-site. Typical turnaround: minutes, not hours — local GPU is significantly faster than waiting for cloud queue position and round-trip network transfer. No internet dependency for this step.
4
Results in cvi42 viewer + PACS export
Analysis results viewable directly in the cvi42 reading interface. Export to PACS as a DICOM SR for review in standard PACS viewer. Physician interprets and issues signed report. Billing triggered from RIS/EHR same as cloud workflow.
IT — Infrastructure requirements
Network & bandwidth
  • →Standard hospital gigabit LAN sufficient for local DICOM transfer
  • →Dedicated internet: 100 Mbps recommended for concurrent cloud submissions
  • →Sizing formula: concurrent_cases × avg_study_GB × 8 ÷ upload_window_min = Mbps. Example: 3 cases × 1.5 GB × 8 ÷ 10 min = 36 Mbps needed; 100 Mbps gives comfortable headroom
  • →Outbound ports: DICOM (104 / 11112) or HTTPS (443) to vendor endpoints
  • →Firewall whitelist: vendor provides specific IP ranges or domain-based allowlist rules
  • →DICOM compression: JPEG 2000 lossless can reduce study size 30–50% — confirm vendor accepts compressed DICOM before enabling
  • →Site-to-site VPN: some vendors offer or require it — confirm during procurement
PACS integration
  • →DICOM AE Title configured in PACS for both send (to vendor) and receive (from vendor) separately
  • →Major PACS tested: GE Centricity, Philips IntelliSpace, Fujifilm Synapse, Sectra, Merge, Intelerad
  • →Philips IntelliSpace and Sectra: native vendor connector programs — ask vendors for validated connector version

IT — Data governance & security
HIPAA BAA — Required clauses
Execute before any PHI is transmitted — no exceptions
A HIPAA Business Associate Agreement (BAA) is legally required before the first DICOM case is sent. A BAA that is missing or that lacks these specific provisions is insufficient.

Require these clauses in writing:
  • Permitted purposes: PHI use limited to AI analysis service delivery only — not model training, marketing, or secondary research
  • Sub-processors: vendor must list all third-party processors (cloud providers, subcontractors); updates require your approval
  • Breach notification: ≤60 days per HIPAA §164.410 — negotiate for 24-hour discovery notification to your CISO as an additional contractual obligation
  • PHI deletion timeline: specify days post-report delivery (e.g., 30 days); confirm this is documented and auditable
  • Audit rights: your institution retains the right to audit vendor HIPAA compliance annually
  • Return of PHI: in the event of contract termination, vendor must return or certifiably destroy all PHI within a defined window (typically 60 days)
Data Residency
Name the specific cloud region in the contract
US institutions: require vendor to contractually name specific cloud regions (e.g., "AWS us-east-1 and us-west-2"). "US data centers" without specific region names is not contractually enforceable.

Ask: do backups replicate cross-region or cross-border? For multi-site systems: confirm all facilities' data stays in same-country data centers.

EU/UK: CE-cleared vendors should have separate GDPR-compliant EU cloud instances — verify this is a separate instance, not a cross-border transfer from a US primary region.

Keya Medical is cloud-only (no on-site option). Confirm US data center region contractually.
PHI Handling & AI Training
Ask explicitly if your PHI trains the model
Most FDA-cleared vendors use inference-only deployments — your DICOM data is processed for analysis but not used to retrain the production AI model. Confirm this in writing: "Is patient data from our institution used to train or fine-tune your production AI model?"

Retention after report: Vendor PHI retention policies vary. Get the specific timeline in the BAA for every vendor — do not rely on verbal assurances.

Research de-identification: before any de-identified data is shared, confirm the vendor's de-id methodology meets HIPAA Safe Harbor or Expert Determination standard — not just "de-identified." This is separate from the clinical deployment BAA and requires a separate agreement.
Security Certifications — Procurement checklist
Read the exceptions, not just the summary
Standard documents to request during procurement:
  • SOC 2 Type II: request report dated within the last 12 months; read the "exceptions" section, not just the auditor summary — availability varies by vendor; confirm directly during procurement
  • ISO 27001: not universal across vendors — request certificate and verify expiry date is current
  • Penetration test: request most recent report and evidence that critical/high findings were remediated; ask for remediation timeline
  • Minimum TLS version: require TLS 1.2 minimum in contract language; TLS 1.3 preferred for new deployments
  • DICOM encryption: confirm vendor uses TLS-wrapped DICOM (DICOM over TLS / DICOMTLS), not plain-text DICOM over the internet
  • Encryption at rest: AES-256 for stored DICOM data and reports
  • Vulnerability management: ask for patching SLA (e.g., critical CVEs patched within 30 days)

IT — Common failure modes and fixes
Failure Root cause Fix
DICOM send succeeds but no report returned Return AE title not configured; vendor has wrong PACS destination IP or port Test round-trip with one de-identified case before go-live. Confirm return DICOM push is a separate DICOM C-Store configured at vendor — distinct from the forward send.
Report in vendor portal only, not in PACS Return DICOM push not configured or firewall blocking inbound on PACS DICOM port Confirm PACS accepts inbound DICOM from vendor IP range; check firewall rules for inbound as well as outbound; configure vendor → PACS separately from PACS → vendor.
Study rejected by vendor (image quality failure) Non-compliant acquisition: wrong reconstruction phase, low contrast HU, high heart rate motion artifact Review acquisition protocol with CT technologists; set up pre-send QC using PACS automated quality check or manual tech review. Most vendors have published acquisition requirements — embed in your CT protocol documentation.
Upload timeout on large studies Bandwidth bottleneck; study size exceeds vendor file size limit Enable DICOM compression (JPEG 2000 lossless — confirm vendor accepts); schedule large batches off-peak; verify dedicated uplink bandwidth is not consumed by other hospital traffic during peak CT hours.
CPT 75580/75577 charges not generating CDM not updated with new CPT codes; ordering workflow not triggering charge in RIS/EHR Revenue cycle IT adds 75580 and 75577 to CDM as chargeable items linked to imaging encounter type. Test full charge capture path with one real case before clinical rollout.
Prior auth denial on submission Payer medical policy not yet updated for Category I code; missing clinical indication in order Appeal with CMS Category I designation letter + clinical indication documentation. Ensure clinical indication is documented at CCTA order time (not added retroactively). For recurring denials by one payer, escalate through your vendor's reimbursement team or submit a formal coverage determination request directly to the payer.
Silent submission failures (no error, no report) DICOM C-Store completed but vendor received corrupted or incomplete study; no error notification back to PACS Implement DICOM submission monitoring: check vendor portal daily for studies with no report after expected turnaround. HeartFlow sends email alerts for submission failures — enable these. Assign a reimbursement coordinator to review submission status daily in the first 90 days.
IT — Implementation & go-live checklist
IT / CISO
  • 01Execute HIPAA BAA with every cloud vendor before any PHI is transmitted — verify it includes permitted-purpose limits, sub-processor list, breach notification ≤60 days, and PHI deletion timeline
  • 02Request and review SOC 2 Type II report (dated within 12 months) — read the exceptions section, not just the auditor summary
  • 03Request most recent penetration test report and confirm critical/high findings were remediated with documented SLA
  • 04Confirm data residency contractually — name the specific cloud region (e.g., AWS us-east-1), not just "US data centers"
  • 05Configure PACS DICOM AE Title for both forward send (PACS → vendor) and return receive (vendor → PACS) — two separate configurations
  • 06Open outbound firewall rules for vendor DICOM/HTTPS endpoints (vendor provides IP allowlist); for on-prem, verify PACS accepts inbound DICOM from cvi42 server
  • 07Run full round-trip test with one de-identified case — confirm DICOM SR appears in PACS viewer, not vendor portal only
  • 08Confirm EHR order/result workflow with Epic/Cerner IT team; request each vendor's current EHR integration documentation before assuming compatibility
  • 09Establish daily monitoring for failed DICOM submissions — a silent failure means no report, a missed clinical result, and lost revenue
  • 10On-prem (Circle CVI): size GPU server per current Circle CVI spec sheet; plan recurring maintenance windows; include cvi42 DB and DICOM output in hospital backup schedule
  • 11Confirm with vendor in writing: is patient PHI used to train or fine-tune the production AI model? Get "inference-only" commitment written into BAA or vendor agreement
  • 12Define user access management policy for vendor web portals: who can submit cases, retrieve reports, and access audit logs — link to your IAM governance process
